🧠 The 4-Step Universal Writing Process

No matter what unit you are working on, every great piece of writing goes through these four phases:

1. Pre-Write

Brainstorming, mind-mapping, and outlining. Never start with a blank page!

2. Draft

Get your ideas down. Don't worry about perfect spelling or grammar yet. Just flow.

3. Revise

Check the big picture. Does it make sense? Is the vocabulary strong? Move sentences around.

4. Edit

The final polish. Check punctuation, capitalization, spelling, and grammar.

Unit 10: Precious Verse ✨

Unlocking the power of poetry, figurative language, and emotional impact.

🧰 The Poet's Toolkit (Essential Elements)

Simile: Comparing two things using "like" or "as" (e.g., Her smile was bright as the sun).

Metaphor: A direct comparison saying one thing IS another (e.g., The classroom was a zoo).

Personification: Giving human traits to non-human things (e.g., The wind whispered in my ear).

Sensory Language: Words that appeal to the 5 senses (Sight, Sound, Smell, Taste, Touch).

Guided Writing

Practice identifying and building the core elements of poetry before writing your own.

  1. Sensory Brainstorming: Work with the provided prompts to list words associated with your five senses regarding a "precious" object or memory.
  2. Crafting Figurative Language: Complete the sentence frames to create powerful similes and metaphors. Move beyond cliches (e.g., instead of "red as a rose", try "red as a warning light").
  3. Structuring Stanzas: Arrange the provided lines of poetry into a logical stanza, paying attention to the rhythm and rhyme scheme (e.g., AABB or ABAB).

Independent Writing Process

Compose an original poem about something deeply valuable or precious to you.

  1. Step 1: Find Your Inspiration (Pre-write) Choose your subject (a family heirloom, a pet, a memory). Write down 10 words that describe how it makes you feel, and 5 words for how it looks/sounds.
  2. Step 2: Choose Your Structure Decide if you will use a strict rhyme scheme (like AABB) or write in Free Verse (no rhyming rules, but relies heavily on strong rhythm and emotion).
  3. Step 3: Draft the Stanzas Write 3 to 4 stanzas (paragraphs of poetry). Dedicate stanza 1 to describing the object, stanza 2 to its history, and stanza 3 to why it matters to you.
  4. Step 4: Inject the Toolkit (Revise) Go back through your draft. Find two places where you used a basic adjective (like "nice" or "old") and replace them with a simile, metaphor, or personification.
  5. Step 5: The Read-Aloud Test (Edit) Read your poem aloud. If you stumble over a line, the rhythm is off. Add or remove syllables until it flows smoothly.

💡 Pro Tips for Unit 10

  • Show, Don't Tell: Don't write "I love my grandma's ring." Write: "The scratched silver band carries the weight of fifty years of holding hands."
  • Rhyme is Optional, Rhythm is Crucial: Don't force a rhyme if it ruins the meaning. A powerful unrhymed line is better than a silly rhyming one.
  • Use Formatting to Your Advantage: You can use line breaks to create pauses or emphasize a specific word.

Unit 11: Reporting Danger 🚨

Mastering journalistic writing, objective tone, and delivering critical facts.

🧰 The Journalist's Toolkit (Essential Elements)

The 5 Ws & H: Who, What, Where, When, Why, and How.

The Inverted Pyramid: Putting the most critical information at the very beginning, and the least important details at the end.

Objective Tone: Sticking to facts. No personal opinions, no "I think" or "I feel".

Direct & Indirect Quotes: Using eyewitness accounts to add credibility to the report.

Guided Writing

Journalism requires stripping away emotion. Guided practice will train you to write like a factual reporter.

  1. Fact Extraction: Extract the 5 Ws from the provided messy witness statements and list them clearly.
  2. Passive Voice Practice: Convert emotional, active sentences into formal, objective passive voice (e.g., Change "The huge fire destroyed the poor family's house" to "The house was destroyed by the fire").
  3. Drafting the Lead: Use the provided facts to write a single, hard-hitting "Lead" (the first paragraph) that summarizes the event immediately.

Independent Writing Process

Write a complete newspaper article reporting on a dangerous event or rescue operation.

  1. Step 1: Fact Creation (Pre-write) Invent the facts for your crisis. Write down exactly: Who was involved, What happened, Where it happened, When it happened, and Why/How it started.
  2. Step 2: The Headline & Placeline Write a short, punchy headline using strong verbs (e.g., "Flash Flood Traps Hikers"). Begin your article with the City and Date in capital letters (e.g., BANGKOK, OCT 12—).
  3. Step 3: The Lead Paragraph In 1 or 2 sentences maximum, deliver the most important facts. (e.g., "Three teenagers were rescued by helicopter late Tuesday night after a sudden flash flood trapped them in the gorge.")
  4. Step 4: Body Paragraphs & Context Provide the background. Explain how the danger escalated. Add a direct quote from an "official" (e.g., Police Chief Smith stated, "It was the hardest rescue we've done all year.")
  5. Step 5: The Tail (Conclusion) End with the current status of the situation or what will happen next (e.g., "The hikers are recovering in the local hospital. The gorge remains closed to the public.")

💡 Pro Tips for Unit 11

  • Short Paragraphs are Key: Newspaper articles use very short paragraphs (1-3 sentences) to make reading fast and easy for the public.
  • Check Your Bias: Don't use emotional adjectives. A "horrifying, terrifying storm" should just be a "severe Category 4 storm." Let the facts be scary, not your adjectives.

Unit 12: Dramatic Relationships 🎭

Bringing characters to life through play scripts, dialogue, and stage directions.

🧰 The Playwright's Toolkit (Essential Elements)

Script Formatting: Character names in ALL CAPS on the left, followed by a colon. No quotation marks used!

Stage Directions: Written in present tense, inside brackets [like this], explaining how actors move or speak.

Dramatic Conflict: The engine of the scene. Character A wants X, Character B wants Y. They cannot both win.

Subtext: The hidden meaning behind the words. What a character is actually feeling but not saying aloud.

Guided Writing

Writing a script is entirely different from writing a story. Guided practice helps you master the format and subtext.

  1. Formatting Practice: Rewrite standard prose into script format. Ensure names are capitalized and colons are used correctly.
  2. Adding Stage Directions: Insert brackets into the provided dialogue to show how the characters move, their facial expressions, or their tone of voice (e.g., [Sighs heavily and turns away]).
  3. Finding the Subtext: Analyze a short scene and answer questions about what the characters are really feeling beneath their simple dialogue.

Independent Writing Process

Write a short, original dramatic scene featuring two characters experiencing a conflict.

  1. Step 1: Character & Conflict (Pre-write) Invent two characters. Give them a relationship (siblings, friends, boss/employee). Decide exactly what the argument is about and what each person secretly wants.
  2. Step 2: Establish the Setting Start your script with a descriptive stage direction setting the scene. (e.g., [A messy kitchen. Midnight. SARAH is pacing the floor. The front door creaks open.])
  3. Step 3: Draft the Dialogue Let them argue. Keep the sentences relatively short. People interrupt each other when they are emotional—use dashes (-) to show interruptions.
  4. Step 4: Layer in Stage Directions (Revise) Go back through your dialogue. Add physical actions. People don't just stand still when they argue; they wash dishes aggressively, avoid eye contact, or pace.
  5. Step 5: The Climax & Resolution The argument must reach a boiling point. Decide if the scene ends with a resolution (they make up) or a dramatic exit (someone storms out).

💡 Pro Tips for Unit 12

  • Read it Aloud with a Friend: The only way to know if dialogue sounds natural is to speak it out loud. If it sounds like a textbook, rewrite it to sound like real human speech.
  • Use Props: Give your characters something to hold or mess with. A character snapping a pencil in half shows anger much better than a line saying "I am angry."
  • The Power of the Pause: Use [Pause] or [Silence] in your stage directions. Silence builds incredible tension on stage.
