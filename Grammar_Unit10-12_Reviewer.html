Unit 10: Precious Verse

1. Future Forms (Simple Future)

The future simple tense is versatile. In poetry, the future simple is often used to show a speaker's determination to continue an action in the future .

  • Refusal: Indicates a strict refusal to do something (e.g., "I will not stop my hunt for treasure.") .
  • Willingness: Showing readiness to help (e.g., "Jack will carry your bag for you.") .
  • Order: Giving a command (e.g., "You will clean your shoes.") .
  • Prediction: Stating a future fact (e.g., "These bottles will be recycled.") .

2. Vocabulary: Collocations

Journalistic language in news reports contains a lot of typical collocations (words that naturally go together) . Paying attention to these can help you write more realistic news reports .

Examples: a stunning turnaround, daring missions, underscoring the danger .

Tip: Reading news articles about events can help you to collect these types of collocation .

Unit 11: Reporting Danger

1. Passive Voice Tenses

When reporting events, it is often more important to explain what happens to people or places (rather than saying what they did); for this, we use the passive voice .

  • Present Simple Passive: "The boys' health is monitored regularly."
  • Past Continuous Passive: "The boys were being given special training."
  • Future Simple Passive: "All the boys will be discharged."

2. Vocabulary: Delexical Verbs

In English, there is a group of very common verbs whose meaning depends almost entirely on what follows them. These verbs are called delexical verbs and are more common in spoken and less formal written English .

Examples: using verbs like take and get. (e.g., "take it in turns", "get tired") .

Unit 12: Dramatic Relationships

1. Question Types

In drama, just like in normal conversation, questions are used for different purposes, depending on the type: for permission, to find something out, as a rhetorical device, etc.

  • Subject question: where the answer is the subject of the sentence (e.g., Who loves you?) .
  • Open question: beginning with a Wh- word .
  • Echo question: where the speaker repeats what he/she has heard (e.g., Light Po-llu-tion?) .
  • Permission question: yes/no question often beginning with a modal verb for permission (e.g., Can I come?) .
  • Tag questions: are formed with a positive (or negative) sentence and a short negative (or positive) question at the end .
  • Rhetorical questions: are used when we don't want or expect an answer (e.g., How should I know?) .
  • Declarative questions: do not use interrogative word order but are in the form of a sentence (e.g., You're taking the bus home?) .

2. Writing Drama: Speech Sounds

When writing drama—that is, spoken words—it is important to match the patterns of spoken language, including sounds (interjections and exclamations) we make to express different ideas without words .
