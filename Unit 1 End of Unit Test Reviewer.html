CONCEPT 1: Verb Tenses & Sequence of Events

Understanding the "When" of a Story

Verbs are the engine of your sentences—they tell us exactly when an action happens. In narrative writing, keeping your timeframes clear is crucial. Always look for time clues in the sentence to choose the correct verb tense.

🔍 Deep Dive: Master the Simple Past Tense

The Simple Past Tense is used for actions that started and completely finished in the past. When reviewing myth or narrative test questions, look out for these three essential components:

1. Sentence Clues (Time Markers):
Look closely for words indicating a completed period, such as:
  • "earlier today" (the action happened this morning or hours ago)
  • "during her time..." (indicates a past era, historic reign, or lifetime)
  • "yesterday", "centuries ago", or "last year"
2. Regular Past Verbs (-ed)

Regular verbs follow a strict rule: just add -ed or -d to the base word.

Examples:
• land → landed
• sigh → sighed
• arrive → arrived
3. Irregular Past Verbs (Spelling Changes)

Irregular verbs throw the rules out the window! You must memorize their completely new shapes.

Examples:
• break → broke
• lead → led
• come → came
⚡ The Narrative Interruption Rule:
Stories frequently use a past action to interrupt a background setting.
Form: [Simple Past Action] happened right in the middle of a [Past Continuous Background Action].
Example: Someone broke (Simple Past) the surfboard while she was wandering (Past Continuous background) around.

🔍 Solved Examples: Analyzing Past Tense Context

Study how the past tense is accurately identified using clues and context structures from your unit text.

Question Pattern: The flight landed earlier today. So we went to the airport and picked them up. Why it's Simple Past: 1. The phrase "earlier today" signals an action that is over. 2. The follow-up sentence uses "went" and "picked," establishing a consistent chain of completed past events.
Question Pattern: Kao’ao sighed when Kalea stormed after hearing his suggestion. Why it's Simple Past: The word "stormed" is already written in the past tense. Because both characters are responding to each other at that exact moment in the story sequence, Kao'ao's verb must match it in the past tense ("sighed").
Question Pattern: Someone broke Kalea’s surfboard when she was wandering around the island. Why it's Simple Past: This applies the Narrative Interruption Rule. Her wandering was an ongoing background state, but the breaking of the board was a quick, singular action that cut right through it. "Broke" is the irregular past form of "break."
Question Pattern: Chicha, a fierce warrior, led the island’s defense during her time as war chief. Why it's Simple Past: The limiting constraint "during her time" lets us know that her entire career as war chief belongs to a completed era of history. "Led" is the irregular past form of "lead."
Question Pattern: By the time her brother arrived, Kalea already woke up after being hit by a huge wave. Why it's Simple Past: The sentence outlines a specific chronological timeline in a myth. "Woke up" shows that an action finished in the past, so the brother's movement toward that scene must also be recorded in the past tense ("arrived").

CONCEPT 2: Capitalization of Titles

Proper Nouns vs. Common Nouns

Capitalization tells the reader when a word refers to a specific, unique name versus a general category. Pay special attention to professional and royal titles.

The Core Rules:
1. Capitalize official titles (Doctor, King) ONLY when attached directly to a name (e.g., Doctor Harrison).
2. Do NOT capitalize if talking generally (e.g., the doctor).
3. Do NOT capitalize plural titles (e.g., the kings).

🔍 Solved Examples: Fixing Capitalization Errors

Test Sentence: The mighty Kings formed an alliance.
Correction: The mighty kings...
Why: Plural and used generally, not with a name.
Test Sentence: The soldiers were treated by doctor Harrison.
Correction: ...treated by Doctor Harrison.
Why: Attached directly to the name "Harrison."

CONCEPT 3: Show, Don't Tell (Action Verbs)

Using Precise Vocabulary for Impact

In creative writing, saying "He was sad" is boring. Good writers use specific Action Verbs to paint a picture of emotion. You must know the exact definitions of these strong verbs.

🔍 Vocabulary Study Guide

Memorize these action verbs and their exact definitions.

Action Verb Definition & Example Use
Groan
Definition: To make a low, rumbling sound of suffering or annoyance.
Example: The injured warrior let out a groan when touched.
Longed
Definition: To desire or wish for something very deeply.
Example: Trapped in the dungeon, the hero longed for sunlight.
Beg
Definition: To ask desperately and earnestly (like charity or mercy).
Example: The captured thief had to beg for forgiveness.
Hurl
Definition: To throw an object with immense physical force.
Example: The giant was strong enough to hurl massive boulders.
Whisper
Definition: To talk in a hushed, quiet tone so others cannot hear.
Example: The spies had to whisper their secret plans.
